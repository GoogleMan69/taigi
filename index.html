Taiwanese Hokkien in Daily Life

An International Student Perspective

Exploring bilingual language use in Taiwan and comparing with overseas Chinese Hokkien varieties

Language Landscape in Taiwan

Official languages: Mandarin Chinese (國語), Taiwanese Hokkien (台語), Hakka (客家話), Indigenous languages

Usage: Mandarin (~94% fluent), Taiwanese Hokkien (~81% fluent)

Reality: Most Taiwanese switch between Mandarin and Hokkien daily

  • Japanese colonial period (1895-1945): Japanese imposed
  • Post-1945: Mandarin dominant in education and government
  • 1990s onwards: Language equality movement

Code-Switching Patterns

🏠 Taiwanese Families

  • With grandparents: Primarily Taiwanese Hokkien
  • With parents: Mixed - start in Mandarin, switch to Hokkien for emotions
  • With siblings: Mostly Mandarin, Hokkien for humor or frustration

🏫 University/Public Spaces

  • Formal settings: Mandarin dominant
  • Casual conversations: Frequent code-switching
  • Example: "老師，我 beh 去 toilet" (Teacher, I want to go toilet)

Language Functions

When Taiwanese Use Hokkien:

  • Emotional expressions: Switch when angry or excited
  • Traditional contexts: Temples, festivals
  • Intimate conversations: Personal topics
  • Cultural identity: Expressing "Taiwanese-ness"

When They Use Mandarin:

  • With international students: Always accommodate
  • Formal education: All classes
  • Professional settings: Work environments
  • Written communication: Official documents

Code-Switching Examples

Common Mixing Patterns:

1. Mandarin frame + Hokkien words:

"我真的很 gâu 煮菜" (I'm really good at cooking)

2. Emotional emphasis:

"你 siánn-mih 意思？" (What do you mean?)

3. Concepts that don't translate:

"真 gâu" (really skilled/clever) - no exact Mandarin equivalent

Our Learning Experience:

  • Initial confusion when conversations switched
  • Friends taught us language choice has emotional meaning
  • Most Taiwanese friends translate patiently for us

Classical Literature & Poetry

Tang Dynasty Poetry in Hokkien:

Tang Dynasty poems (618-907 CE) preserve better rhyme schemes when read in Hokkien

李白 - "靜夜思" (Quiet Night Thoughts)

床前明月光 (Chông-chiáⁿ bêng-goe̍h-kong)

疑是地上霜 (Gî-sī tē-siōng-song)

舉頭望明月 (Kí-thâu bōng bêng-goe̍h)

低頭思故鄉 (Tê-thâu su kò͘-hiong)

Perfect rhymes: kong/song/goe̍h/hiong - lost in modern Mandarin

Modern Movement: Taiwan is currently the only country with a literary circle of Hokkien poets

Global Context

Overseas Chinese Hokkien Varieties:

  • Malaysian Hokkien: Community preservation language
  • Indonesian Hokkien: Historical influence, now declining
  • Singaporean Hokkien: Part of multilingual mixing
  • Taiwanese Hokkien: Regional identity language

As international students, we wanted to understand how Taiwan's bilingual situation compares to other countries and what different patterns of language mixing exist worldwide.

Malaysian Hokkien

Similarities to Taiwanese:

  • Basic vocabulary and grammar structure
  • Tone system (though slight variations)

Key Differences:

  • Loanwords: Heavy Malay influence (cari = 找, makan = 吃)
  • Code-switching: Mixed with Malay, English, Mandarin, Cantonese
  • Usage context: Community preservation vs. regional identity
  • Example: "Lu mau pi mana?" (Where do you want to go?) - Malay syntax influence

Indonesian & Singaporean Hokkien

Indonesian Hokkien:

  • Hokkien merchants traded in Southeast Asia since 10th century
  • Almost all Chinese loanwords in Indonesian come from Hokkien
  • Examples: mie (麵), lumpia (潤餅), teko (茶壺)
  • Currently: Mainly elderly speakers, declining transmission

Singaporean Hokkien:

  • Multilingual mixing with English, Mandarin, Malay
  • Part of "Singlish" integration
  • Example: "Wah, sibeh expensive leh!" (Wow, very expensive!)
  • Discouraged by government vs. Taiwan's recent promotion

Cultural & Identity Functions

Taiwan - Regional Identity & Literary Renaissance:

  • Political significance: Democracy movement, local identity
  • Cultural revival: Pop music, literature, theater in Hokkien
  • Educational support: Hokkien classes in schools
  • Literary achievement: Only country with active Hokkien literary circle

Overseas - Ethnic Preservation:

  • Heritage maintenance: Connecting to ancestral roots
  • Community bonding: In-group solidarity
  • Declining vitality: Younger generations shifting to local languages
  • Limited literary development: Contrast with Taiwan's renaissance

Current Trends & Future

Taiwan Developments:

  • Government support: Mother tongue education policies
  • Digital presence: Hokkien input methods, online content
  • Pop culture: Young artists using Hokkien creatively
  • Academic recognition: University courses, research programs

Overseas Challenges:

  • Language shift: Younger generations preferring local languages
  • Limited resources: Few educational materials or media
  • Identity evolution: Integration vs. heritage preservation
  • No literary movements: Unlike Taiwan's thriving scene

Our International Student Perspective

What Surprised Us:

  • Seamless switching: How naturally Taiwanese people code-switch
  • Emotional significance: Language choice carries meaning
  • Generational patterns: Age affects language preference
  • Literary culture: Taiwan's unique Hokkien poetry scene
  • Accommodation: Quick switch to Mandarin for foreigners

Our Challenges:

  • Language barrier: Challenging when groups switch to Hokkien
  • Social inclusion: Sometimes feel left out
  • Cultural understanding: Missing embedded references
  • Learning decision: Should we invest time in Hokkien?

Key Takeaways

  • Regional Identity: Hokkien functions as identity marker alongside Mandarin
  • Literary Uniqueness: Taiwan is the only place with active Hokkien literary culture
  • Overseas Preservation: Serves ethnic community maintenance with local influences
  • Natural Bilingualism: Code-switching is functional, not deficient
  • Cultural Significance: Language choice carries identity and emotional meaning
  • International Insight: Observing bilingualism offers cultural understanding
